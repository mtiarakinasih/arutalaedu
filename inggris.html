Owls are distinctive birds that come in many shapes, sizes, and colors. They all belong to one of two scientific bird family classifications. The Strigidae owls are the typical or true owls, which include the majority of owl species. The Tytonidae owls, or barn owls, have more typically counter-shaded feathers and heart-shaped facial disks. Owls have many physical characteristics that make them distinct from other birds. An owl’s face is uniquely shaped with a disk-like structure surrounding the bill and eyes, which the bird can use to recognize surrounding sounds. The exact shape and color of the facial disk are great field marks for identifying owls. Owls have forward-facing eyes that give them binocular vision, improving depth perception and the ability to judge distances. The ear tufts, which are the longer feathers on the side of the head, look like ears or horns, though they are not associated with hearing in any way. Not all owls have ear tufts, but it is believed that the tufts can help with the bird’s camouflage by breaking up its outline to blend in better with its environment. The position of the tufts can also serve as a form of communication between owls. Owls are carnivorous and eat a wide variety of animals, including rodents, insects, fish, and larger mammals.
